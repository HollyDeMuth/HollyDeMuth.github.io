Beach Closures in the Rockaways Leave Surfers Grounded



A rescheduled surf competition has some locals asking questions


ROCKAWAY BEACH, NY
9.9.19

BY HOLLY DEMUTH

It was the Saturday after Labor Day, and the surfers gathered at Rockaway Beach thought it was perfect surfing weather. Unfortunately for them, NYC Parks did not agree.

Beaches citywide were closed on Friday, Sept. 6, and Saturday, Sept. 7, due to concerns about unsafe conditions caused by Hurricane Dorian. The closures meant postponing the 4th Annual Rockaway Beach Bodysurf Contest, which was set to draw up to 72 surfers, many of them from out of state.

“The ocean is not a swimming pool. That is what makes the ocean so amazing. It should not be controlled as if it was a swimming pool.”

Local residents expressed dismay about the closures, contending that they are part of a pattern of knee-jerk reactions on the part of city officials who have little experience with the ocean. “The authority that runs the beach is completely out of step with what is happening on the sand and inthe water,”said local teacher and surfer Clare Hilger during public comments at a Community Board Meeting on Sept. 10. Hilger, who was attending the meeting as a representative of Rock-away Beach Civic Association (RBCA), argued that such closures do nothing to make beachgo-ers safer.

Representatives from the beach association contended that decisions to close beaches are not based on good science. They argued for a dedicated ocean commissioner, someone with experience in oceanography or a related field who could make educated decisions about closures. “Surfers and ocean swimmers study the water conditions,” said Hilger. “Those who run the beach seem to understand very little about how to regularly read these conditions or participate at all in the environment that they are supposed to support.”

Local oceanographer Dr. Philip Orton, who surfs himself, said he understood the surfers’ frustration. “It’s crazy when you tell surfers they can’t go in the water when there’s a swell,” said the scientist. He speculated that NYC Parks administrators were concerned about the prospect of rip tides, acknowledging that a hurricane swell would likely worsen that risk. Such currents could be dangerous for swimmers, but Orton said he has never understood closing beaches for surfers as well. “When you’re on a surfboard, a rip current’s not a big deal,” he said. “You don’t see surfers dying very often going out into waves. That’s what they do.”

NYC Parks, for their part, contend that the closures were necessary. “We make decisions about beach closures based on overall safety concerns, given the conditions and forecasts,” said Eric Peterson, Rockaway Administrator for NYC Parks, via email. “We have to err on the side of caution, taking into account the wide range of abilities of swimmers and surfers who use the beaches.”

With four drowning deaths this summer, water safety remains a concern in the Rockaways. Hilger argues that the way to combat such deaths is through education, not automatic closures. She contends that a comprehensive water safety program that teaches people to swim safely in the ocean would do more to prevent such deaths than closing beaches. “The ocean is not a swimming pool. That is what makes the ocean so amazing,” said Hilger. “It should not be controlled as if it was a swimming pool."
